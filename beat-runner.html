The Challenge

Design a rhythm game that feels great to play, works with any music, and includes full progression and monetization systems‚Äîall while being technically feasible as a web-based 3D experience.

Most rhythm games rely on pre-charted content, which limits music selection and requires significant manual work. I wanted to create something that could adapt to any song automatically while maintaining tight, satisfying gameplay.

My Role & Design Approach

As the sole designer and developer, I handled everything from initial concept to final implementation:

  • Core Loop Design: Defined lane-based movement, rhythm timing windows, and obstacle variety
  • Progression System: Created 30-level structure with difficulty curve and star-based unlocks
  • Economy Design: Designed Orbs (soft currency) earning rate and cosmetic pricing
  • Technical Implementation: Built the game in Three.js with BPM detection and sync
  • UI/UX: Designed clean, game-feel focused interface with neon aesthetic

Key Design Decisions

1. Three Lanes, Not Five

Initial Design: Started with 5 lanes to maximize variety and challenge.

Problem Discovered: Playtesters struggled with peripheral vision and made frequent input errors. The game felt chaotic rather than challenging.

Solution: Reduced to 3 lanes. This simplified visual tracking while still allowing for interesting obstacle patterns. Added vertical variation (obstacles at different heights) to maintain complexity without overwhelming players.

Result: Much better player feedback. The game felt "tight" and responsive rather than unfair.

2. Visual Beat Indicators

Problem: Players without strong rhythm sense struggled to time their movements, especially when learning new songs.

Solution: Added pulsing lane highlights that flash on the beat. This gives players a visual cue to sync with the music without dictating exact actions.

Design Principle: Help players succeed without removing the challenge. The indicators show when to act, but players still choose what to do.

3. Two Game Modes for Different Player Mindsets

Stage Mode: 30 handcrafted levels with specific difficulty curves, star ratings, and unlock progression. Appeals to achievement-oriented players who want clear goals.

Free Run Mode: Upload any song and play infinitely. Appeals to players who want to zone out with their own music. No pressure, pure flow state.

Why Both Matter: Different sessions call for different experiences. After a stressful day, players want Free Run. When they want to improve, they pick Stage Mode.

4. Orbs as Engagement, Not Monetization Blocker

Economy Design: Orbs (soft currency) are earned generously through gameplay. Players collect them by hitting obstacles perfectly.

Cosmetic Shop: Two ball skins available (Disco Ball, Fire Ball). Prices are achievable within 3-5 runs, ensuring players can unlock content through play.

Design Philosophy: The shop exists to give players goals and variety, not to frustrate them. In a real F2P implementation, I'd add premium currency for faster unlocks, but the core experience remains free.

  • Audio Analysis: Web Audio API for real-time BPM detection and beat tracking
  • Procedural Generation: Dynamic obstacle spawning based on music tempo and difficulty level

  • State Management: Clean save/load system for progression, unlocks, and player settings

What I Learned

Player Fantasy > Feature Count

Initially planned 10+ power-ups and complex combo systems. After testing, realized the core fantasy is "moving to music." Removed distractions and focused on making the basic loop feel amazing.

Difficulty Curves Are Critical

Early levels were too hard. Players quit before experiencing the "flow state" that makes rhythm games addictive. Adjusted the first 5 levels to be significantly easier‚Äîretention improved dramatically.

Visual Feedback Sells Game Feel

Added screen shake, particle bursts, and sound effects when hitting obstacles. These "juice" elements transformed the game from functional to fun. Small polish details have outsized impact on perception.
